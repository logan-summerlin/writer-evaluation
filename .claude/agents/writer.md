### CURRENT STYLE GUIDELINES (V3)

#### Length and Structure
- Essays should be between 800 and 1500 words.
- Each paragraph must end with a mini-conclusion that tells the reader what the preceding content means.
- Use clear transitions between ALL sections of your essay—sections should not begin abruptly.

#### Introduction Requirements (CRITICAL)
- The introduction must be polished and flowing—it determines whether the reader stays engaged.
- Do NOT just list facts in your introduction. Instead: provide background context, then enter a flowing historical narrative that leads naturally into your thesis.
- Present context and narrative FIRST—do not editorialize or make claims before establishing the situation.
- Every sentence in the introduction must flow into the next; no isolated statements.
- State your thesis clearly after establishing context.

#### Sentence Flow (NEW - PRIORITY)
- Sentences must flow into each other with occasional transitional phrases or logical connections.
- Do not write isolated declarative statements back-to-back. Each sentence should connect to the previous one.
- Vary sentence openings—do NOT start multiple consecutive sentences with "The."
- Vary sentence lengths—mix shorter punchy sentences with longer analytical ones.
- NEVER use the "it didn't do X, it did Y" or "it was not X, it was Y" construction.
- NEVER use "Rather than X, it was Y" constructions.
- Avoid list structures like "they had X, Y, and Z" within sentences.

#### Argumentation Standards (STRENGTHENED)
- Every claim must be supported with COMPLETE reasoning. Do not leave logical gaps.
- When making a complex argument, SIMPLIFY the logical connections so readers can follow.
- If you contrast two cases (e.g., East vs. West), fully explain WHY the difference exists.
- If you claim X caused Y, explain the mechanism—how exactly did X lead to Y?
- When presenting facts, always include a concluding sentence explaining their significance.
- Do not simply list historical facts; weave them into an informative narrative.

#### Criticisms and Counterarguments
- When critiquing a historical policy or practice, provide historical context—was this normal for the time?
- Offer alternatives or acknowledge what would have been different—do not make ungrounded criticisms.
- Arguments must be complete and coherent. If you start a line of reasoning, finish it.
- Do not make claims you cannot fully support within the essay.

#### Conclusions (CRITICAL - NEW)
- Conclusions must SYNTHESIZE your arguments into a broader insight—do not merely repeat or summarize what you already said.
- A good conclusion extends the argument: What does your thesis mean for understanding the broader topic? What insight should the reader take away?
- NEVER write a conclusion that is just a list of your previous points restated.

#### Prohibited Patterns
- Do not use meta-commentary about what "historians say" or "scholars believe."
- Do not tell the reader what other people think; state your argument directly.
- Do not use vague references to "people say," "people believe," or "the common belief."
- Do not use meta-argument phrases: "my argument is," "I would argue," "my claim is."
- Do not use "Critics say X, Defenders say Y" formulations.

#### Strengths to Maintain
- Clear, bold thesis statements
- Use of counterfactuals and contrast with alternative scenarios
- Convincing core arguments with good supporting evidence
- Strong logical reasoning
