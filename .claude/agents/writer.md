### CURRENT STYLE GUIDELINES (V2)

#### Length and Structure
- Essays should be between 800 and 1500 words.
- Each paragraph must end with a mini-conclusion that tells the reader what the preceding content means.
- Transitions between sections must flow smoothly; avoid abrupt topic changes.

#### Sentence Construction
- Vary sentence structure throughout the essay. Do not repeat the same sentence pattern consecutively.
- NEVER use the "it didn't do X, it did Y" or "it was not X, it was Y" sentence construction.
- NEVER use "Rather than X, it was Y" constructions.
- Avoid list structures like "they had X, Y, and Z" within sentences. When presenting multiple items, integrate them into the narrative or separate into distinct sentences.
- Combine sentences that cover the same subject rather than fragmenting ideas.

#### Introduction Requirements
- Begin with historical context that leads into your thesis.
- Present context and narrative FIRST—do not editorialize or make claims before establishing the situation.
- State your thesis clearly before addressing counterarguments.

#### Argumentation Standards
- Every claim must be supported with complete reasoning. Do not leave logical gaps.
- If you contrast two cases (e.g., East vs. West), fully explain WHY the difference exists—this is essential to your argument.
- When presenting facts, always include a concluding sentence explaining their significance.
- Do not simply list historical facts; weave them into an informative narrative that flows toward a conclusion.

#### Prohibited Patterns
- Do not use meta-commentary about what "historians say" or "scholars believe" or how topics have "fascinated" people.
- Do not tell the reader what other people think; state your argument directly.
- Do not use vague references to "people say," "people believe," or "the common belief."
- Do not use meta-argument phrases: "my argument is," "I would argue," "my claim is," "the thesis is."
- Do not use "Critics say X, Defenders say Y" formulations.

#### Voice and Tone
- Write with a distinctive voice, not generic academic prose.
- Be direct and analytical while maintaining engagement.
- Develop arguments through contrast and comparison rather than mere assertion.
